Why Injuries Happen

The Three Root Causes of Most Injuries

Experts estimate that the vast majority of physical injuries trace back to the same three preventable factors — all of which AIS directly addresses.

01

Fatigue

Accumulated physical and neuromuscular fatigue impairs coordination, reduces reaction time, and leaves tissues vulnerable to stress beyond their capacity. Recovery through targeted stretching restores tissue resilience.

02

Excess Muscle Tension

Chronic hypertonicity restricts blood flow and oxygen delivery to muscle tissue, creating a cycle of tension, pain, and compensatory movement patterns that increase injury risk with every repetition.

03

Lack of Flexibility

Reduced range of motion forces joints and surrounding soft tissue to absorb forces they were never designed to handle alone. Systematic flexibility work rebuilds the body's natural shock-absorption capacity.

The Science

Why Static Stretching Falls Short

For decades, holding a stretch for 60 seconds was considered the gold standard. New research — and a deeper understanding of physiology — tells a very different story.

Prolonged Static Stretching 60-second holds — the old standard
Decreases blood flow within tissue, starving cells of oxygen
Creates localized ischemia and lactic acid buildup in muscle fibers
Triggers protective contraction via the myotatic (stretch) reflex — working against itself
May irritate or damage muscular, tendinous, lymphatic, and neural tissues
Similar in consequence to overuse syndromes and microtrauma
VS
Active Isolated Stretching (AIS) 2-second holds — the Mattes Method
Maintains and improves blood flow and oxygen delivery to working tissues
Prevents lactic acid accumulation and promotes lymphatic drainage
Works with the body's reciprocal inhibition reflex — the target muscle releases naturally
Restores fascial integrity at both superficial and deep planes without irritation
Safe for post-surgical recovery, chronic conditions, and high-performance training
